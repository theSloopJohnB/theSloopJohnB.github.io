MARO PARENTS

Intro

Maro Parents is a mobile app built to help parents recognize mental health challenges in their child early and get the support they need through easy access to a doctor and educational content written by licensed professionals. My team was tasked with creating an effective, intuitive way for parents to sign up, navigate the home screen, learn, journal, track their child’s behavior, review past entries, and navigate their child’s care.

My role in the project involved conducting extensive user research, performing competitive and heuristic analysis, wireframing, conducting user testing, and prototyping to completely redesign a mobile app for parents on behalf of Maro, a leading EdTech organization.

My role: UX Designer

Team: I worked with three other User Experience Designers Jeanine Reeman, Drew Miller, and Melanie Martin

Timeline: January - April 2023

My Role

I started with a heuristic analysis to understand all the features of the app in its current state. I also conducted comparative analysis to consider how various other platforms handle the flow of similar tasks. Maro’s business goals were gaining investor support and encouraging their users to gain insights from inputting data. Key things I wanted to improve upon were the conversion rate (of free to paid users) and user engagement (number of active users), as well as limited user research that had been performed already. My primary goal was designing an app to best suit the needs and expectations of parents.

”How Might We” statement

How might we redesign the Maro Parents app to increase user engagement and gain support from investors while addressing scalability?

Research and Data Synthesis

Heuristic Analysis

I analyzed every flow within the app, from onboarding to settings, but my key findings centered around identifying accessibility issues (such as insufficient contrast between text color and background color) and inconsistent interfaces that should be using the same components. I also identified many opportunities to incorporate common design patterns, like search functionality and other components that would improve the content browsing experience.

Comparative Analysis

I analyzed a wide range of apps in a variety of spaces since Maro Parents has many different features. I ended up focusing on emulating the way that Duolingo uses gamification to keep the user engaged in learning, the way that Flo makes data entry and personalized content feel intuitive and attention-grabbing, and the way that Headspace uses delightful graphics to spark joy and teach the user about difficult topics.

User Interviews

The research phase included interviewing 6 parents, scattered geographically around the United States and raising kids between the ages of 0 and 11. My research was broad and generative but also involved targeted discussions around parents’ habits related to seeking out content, trusting apps, and tracking things about their children.

Key Findings

  • Parents believe that pediatricians and medical doctors do not know enough about mental health to provide families with the support they need. (This was mentioned by 5 out of 6 parents interviewed.)
  • Parents seek out information through community and they are avid researchers. They especially seek out information when concerned about developmental milestones/health issues, to learn about parenting techniques, and for reassurance and advice. 5 out of 6 parents interviewed mentioned using an online community as a means of support.
  • Parents tend to track patterns in earlier developmental stages and this becomes less frequent as their child gets older unless they have a specific concern. When they do seek out tracking tools, they look for ones that are flexible and familiar, since 3 out of 6 parents were simply using their phone’s inbuilt Notes app to track behaviors.
  • Parents are highly influenced by their peers and by society. Parents tend to trust products tied to institutions, and they trust parenting content written by people with therapy or academic backgrounds or relevant lived experience.
  • Parents want to know what their data is being used for and why their information is being requested. Parents do not mind sharing sensitive information with health professionals through patient portals.

Affinity Mapping

User Persona

The next step was to put together a user persona based on the findings gleaned from user interviews. This would inform the app design going forward.

Direction of Design

  • I was able to validate the driving principle behind Maro’s parenting platform. The need for early intervention and awareness around kids’ mental health was highlighted by our research finding that parents do not feel that medical doctors adequately offer the mental health support they need.
  • I realized that a journaling feature would have to feel seamless and simple for parents to use it rather than their phone’s inbuilt Notes app.
  • I deduced that content in the Maro Parents app should display information about who wrote it and what their background is, and it should be personalized so that data tracking and recommended content could be tailored to the users’ expressed needs, which would also necessitate a more guided and instructive onboarding process and more intuitive navigation throughout. I determined that gamification would keep users engaged with content so parents would be more likely to recommend the app to friends. I also found that users would value a community aspect in the app, so that users could find connections related to their parenting experiences.
  • I concluded that having the option to contact a pediatrician through Maro’s mobile app would be a popular feature for parents.

User Testing

With our user research in mind, I created low-fidelity wireframes and then conducted user testing. The methodology involved asking five users to sign up for the app, create a journal entry documenting data about their child, navigate through a module of learning material, find and filter articles about parenting, and submit a question for parenting experts. This process was deeply impactful and prompted numerous design changes, including redoing the design of the module interface, adding sub-goals and metrics related to the goals parents set, and adding customization options for journaling data. It also led us to make post-module self-reflections more interactive tools that featured additional expandable content. User responses also triggered a complete reconsideration of how we would integrate community sourcing into the content and how we would set up the navigation within the "Explore" tab.

Extra Iteration/Testing Phase

After re-designing the “Explore” tab to integrate the community-sourced questions with the articles, our team needed an extra round of testing to validate such a major change. The new design involved adding an overlay within a hamburger menu so the user could navigate within the “Explore” tab. I used graphics to make clear that the two content types were different and filters to allow the user to segment the content types if they chose. The additional user testing confirmed that this new design made sense and felt familiar.

Problems Addressed by the App Redesign

Onboarding

I was able to vastly improve the sign-up process, making it feel not only smooth and guided but also adding clarity to the questions being asked, why they were being asked, and how many steps were left in the process.

Navigating through articles and modules

Before my redesign, the user would browse articles and learning modules all together within one tab. I decided these two types of content should not be merged, since parents looking to learn in-depth about a topic are looking for something different than parents looking to browse through articles. The segmentation also allowed for the introduction of gamification into the "Learn" tab and the emphasis on recommended and community-sourced content (renamed from "Maro Talk" to "Expert FAQ" and then to "Community Sourced Questions", based on user feedback) in the "Explore" tab.

Journaling

Before, users would enter information about their child’s day one question at a time, without knowing how many questions were left, increasing the likelihood that parents would abandon the process. I instead put the data entry all on one page and replaced much of the data entry with a chip selection structure grouped into categories and featuring illustrative icons. I consulted with various Maro's data and health care teams to ensure data entry steps were optimized to fit their needs.

Other improvements

The design gives the user a stronger sense of the usefulness of setting goals and inputting information for the sake of receiving curated content recommendations. Also, we built the app using a Style Tile developed in coordination with the teams working on other Maro products (websites oriented towards teachers and school counselors), so that all three products therefore have the same strong brand identity.

Reflections

The design process is not perfectly linear. Even after I thought I had worked out kinks in user testing, I had to go back to the drawing board for how content should be structured within the “Explore” section of the app.

Let constraints be a spark for creativity. The Maro team informed us that they did not presently have the resources to devote to content moderation, which limited the ability to add community features such as commenting. However, I ended up implementing an elegant design solution that was perhaps better than what I might have come up with without real-world limitations. The focus on submitting and voting on questions lets users feel less alone as parents without introducing the complexities of an online forum, and embedding these questions among the existing article content makes this new content type easily discoverable.

Don’t be afraid to build out the beginnings of a new aspect of an app, as long as it adds value and is scalable. The community-sourced questions for experts added a much-demanded “community feel” to the app, and users responded that the author pages I designed made them find the content more trustworthy. These structures would be fairly simple to populate since they only involve Maro collecting writers' bios and creating a workflow for the writers to regularly answer community-sourced questions. I remained realistic and communicative with the client about what could be built within a 10-week time frame. I advised them to look to the future to design other features, such as adding interactive dashboards for parents to review journal data, building a doctor chat feature that integrates well with various Electronic Medical Records systems, fits with the terminology of special education plans, and complies with multiple state requirements.

Don't neglect building a brand identity, but still be practical about what developers can build. My design emphasized cute, evocative illustrations (featuring yellow, the Maro brand color) rather than relying on micro-interactions, avatars, and animations that developers would find hard to build and to reliably load. Especially since this design involved building out and improving upon so many different features, I wanted to ensure that incorporating brand identity and interspersing the app with colorful sparks of joy would not require too much heavy lifting.
